It’s a warm Sunday afternoon in Olney, Maryland, as would-be homeowners peruse a three-bedroom townhouse overlooking a lake. This particular home is up for sale for the ripe price of nearly a quarter of a million dollars – which is enough to make prospective first-time homebuyers like Vincent Kiatta think twice.

“The market is tough," Kiatta, 27, said after touring that townhome.

Plenty of Marylanders agree. According to a poll conducted by YouGov Blue for Greater Greater Washington, over 90% of Maryland residents polled think the cost of buying or renting a house is a problem.

Dan Reed is the Maryland policy director of Greater Greater Washington, whose organization helped conduct the poll. Reed said that simply put, making homes more affordable is a necessity for Marylanders.

“People need access to homes they can afford to have,” Reed said.

According to a report released by the Maryland Comptroller’s Office, the average home price in the state is $446,400. But in Montgomery County, one of the more affluent counties in the state, home prices average over $150,000 higher than that, according to the Greater Capital Area Association of Realtors.

So for Reed, who grew up in Montgomery County, the issue of affordable housing hits home.

[PULL QUOTE] “I watched my parents struggle to find homes they could afford growing up,” Reed said. “I lived in a variety of not so great housing situations as a young adult… so this is deeply personal to me as well.”

Kelly Virbickas is a real estate agent with Compass Real Estate, mainly working in the Chevy Chase region – where, she said the average home price is around $1.2 million. Virbickas said a large part of that is for the land you’re getting.

“It's all in the location and the land,” Virbickas said. “People want those locations because of the schooling, the proximity to work, the prestige.”

But the Comptroller's report highlights a bigger problem: there is not enough housing available to meet the demand.

So why can the state simply just build more homes?

The process of building a home is extremely complex, from construction to permitting. Reed said that while there is enough room to build housing, zoning restrictions can slow down the process.

“Many communities in Maryland… require… that on most of the land in that community, you can only build a single family house in a large lot,” Reed said. “But… people who can't afford a large house in a large lot or do not want a large house in a large lot… can't really… access those places.”

Maryland Governor Wes Moore has introduced legislation that would support housing, including the Starter and Silver Homes Act, which would allow for smaller single-family homes on smaller lots and townhouses in residential areas across the state.

But it’s not just about building more homes -- it’s about building enough homes.

The Comptroller’s report said Maryland will need about 590,000 more housing units than it has to meet growth projections through 2045. That’s a number of housing units nearly equal to the entire current population of Wyoming.

So are there enough homes being built to keep Marylanders in the state?

“Not even close,” said Jake Day, secretary of the Maryland Department of Housing and Community Development.

According to the Comptroller’s report, Maryland currently approves between 16,000-19,000 permits annually. In order to meet the state's projected housing goals, the state would have to double the amount of permits they approve.

But going from permitting to building can be painfully slow. Kiatta knows this firsthand.

Kiatta works at Erickson Senior Living, a retirement community with multiple locations in the area. He said the company’s latest construction project in Clarksville has just now gotten under way because of the complicated approval process from the state.

“We've owned the land for five years, and we just broke ground this month on the land,” Kiatta said. “That's how much time it takes.”

Kiatta was touring the townhome in Olney with his girlfriend and parents. He has lived at home his whole life despite being out of college for nearly four years, in large part because of the high cost of housing.

“But… we have to leave Mom and Dad at some point,” he noted.

Some residents are doing more than just leaving mom and dad. They’re leaving the state altogether.

According to the Comptroller’s report, since 2011, Maryland has consistently lost more residents to other states than it has gained, with the most severe losses occurring post-pandemic.

As a lifelong Marylander himself, Day believes that Maryland is still a great place to live and raise a family.

“Maryland has a rich landscape of lovable places,” Day said.“My efforts… around housing affordability and housing supply are really just… to make that possible for more people who want to call Maryland home.”

Reed would agree with that sentiment.

“My family is here. My friends are here. I went to college here,” Reed said. “I wanted to stay here.”

[TO CHANGE] “This area… is a combination of great living and great working,” said Jackie Bennett, the president-elect of the Greater Capital Area Realtors Association.

But if you’re still on the fence about entering this current housing market, Virbickas would say to do it anyway if you can afford to do so.

“I do believe everybody should try and own a home,” Virbickas said.“It's a big expense, but it's usually very rewarding.”
